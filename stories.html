Chinese
Oral History

✦

In recent years, a new wave of Chinese immigrants has made Sudbury home — arriving as students, skilled workers, and families, drawn by Laurentian University, by employment in the mining sector, and by the quieter pull of a city that is still finding its shape. This project gathers their voices. Not as data. Not as case studies. As stories — of arrival, of winter, of learning a place from scratch, of building a life somewhere no one back home has ever heard of.

✦

New Arrivals,
Northern City

✦

Most of our interviewees arrived in Sudbury within the last five to ten years. Some came directly from China; others had spent years in Toronto or Vancouver before moving north for work, for affordability, for a slower pace. Sudbury was rarely the plan — it became the plan. That gap between expectation and reality is where most of these stories live.

The city they describe is cold, sometimes lonely, and genuinely surprising. A place where the Canadian Shield breaks through the ground in the middle of neighbourhoods. Where a Chinese grocery might be a forty-minute drive away. Where the people, once you get past the surface, turn out to be warm in ways that catch you off guard. These are stories about learning to read a place — and slowly, unexpectedly, beginning to belong to it.

✦

"No one told me how long
the winter lasts here.
By March I understood."

Our interviews were conducted at sites across Sudbury that carry meaning for the community — a university campus where many first set foot in the city, a lakeside park where families gather on warm weekends, a library that became an unlikely anchor for people still learning the language.

Each recording is between one and three hours long. We spoke in Mandarin, Cantonese, and English — sometimes all three in the same conversation. The transcripts and subtitled video recordings will be archived and made available to future researchers and to the community itself.

What Gets
Left Behind

✦

Immigration is always also a story of absence — of what could not fit into a suitcase, of the festivals observed alone, of video calls that never quite close the distance. Many of our interviewees spoke about the particular weight of being far from ageing parents, of raising children who will grow up without grandparents nearby, of celebrating the Lunar New Year in a city where the date passes unremarked. These are not complaints. They are the texture of a life lived between two places — and the slow, ongoing work of making one of them feel like home.
